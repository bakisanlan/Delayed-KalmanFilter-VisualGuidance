\begin{abstract}
This paper presents a comprehensive implementation of an Error-State Kalman Filter (ESKF) for visual guidance applications in autonomous interceptor systems. The system fuses high-rate Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) data with low-rate camera and radar measurements to achieve robust state estimation in dynamic 3D scenarios. We develop a complete mathematical formulation of the 17-dimensional error state dynamics, including the interaction matrix for image-based visual servoing (IBVS) features. Additionally, we present an interactive MATLAB Graphical User Interface (GUI) that enables real-time simulation, parameter tuning, and visualization of filter performance. The GUI serves both as an educational tool for understanding ESKF behavior and as a practical testbed for algorithm development. Experimental results demonstrate the filter's ability to accurately estimate relative position, velocity, attitude, and sensor biases under various noise conditions and initial uncertainties.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Motivation and Problem Statement}

Autonomous navigation and target tracking represent fundamental challenges in modern robotics, with applications ranging from autonomous vehicles to unmanned aerial systems (UAS) and guided interceptors. In these applications, accurate real-time estimation of the platform's state---including position, velocity, and attitude---is critical for successful mission execution. The problem becomes particularly challenging when the platform must track a moving target while relying on onboard sensors that have inherent limitations in terms of noise, bias, and update rates.

The specific problem addressed in this work is the state estimation for an autonomous interceptor pursuing a target in three-dimensional space. The interceptor is equipped with a typical sensor suite consisting of an Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) for proprioceptive sensing, a camera for visual target tracking, and a radar for range measurements. Each sensor presents unique characteristics and challenges that must be addressed by the estimation algorithm.

The IMU provides high-rate measurements of angular velocity and specific force, enabling continuous state propagation through dead-reckoning. However, IMU measurements are corrupted by both white noise and slowly-varying biases that cause the dead-reckoning solution to drift unboundedly over time. This drift must be corrected by incorporating exteroceptive measurements from the camera and radar.

The camera provides measurements of the target's position in the image plane, yielding information about the line-of-sight direction to the target. These measurements are available at a moderate rate (typically 30 Hz) and may be subject to processing delays. The normalized image coordinates are nonlinearly related to the relative position and attitude, requiring careful treatment in the estimation framework.

The radar provides direct measurements of the relative position vector, but at a much lower update rate (typically 0.5-2 Hz). While less frequent, these measurements are valuable for correcting accumulated drift in the position estimate.

\subsection{Error-State Kalman Filter Approach}

The choice of estimation algorithm is crucial for achieving robust performance in this multi-sensor fusion scenario. Traditional Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) formulations that directly estimate the quaternion attitude suffer from several drawbacks, including the need to explicitly enforce quaternion unit-norm constraints and potential numerical issues arising from the four-dimensional quaternion representation.

The Error-State Kalman Filter (ESKF), also known as the Multiplicative Extended Kalman Filter (MEKF) in the attitude estimation literature, addresses these challenges by separating the state representation into a nominal state and an error state. The nominal state follows the standard nonlinear kinematics using quaternion algebra, naturally preserving the unit-norm constraint. The error state, which is what the Kalman filter actually estimates, uses a minimal three-parameter attitude representation (such as axis-angle or rotation vector), eliminating redundancy and avoiding constraint enforcement issues.

The key advantages of the ESKF formulation are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Natural quaternion handling:} The quaternion evolves on its natural manifold through proper quaternion multiplication, automatically maintaining the unit-norm constraint.
    \item \textbf{Minimal state dimension:} The error state uses only 17 dimensions compared to 18 for the nominal state, reducing computational cost and avoiding singularities associated with over-parameterization.
    \item \textbf{Better linearization:} Since the error state is always reset to zero after each measurement update, the linearization is always performed around zero error, which is typically close to the true error, improving filter consistency.
    \item \textbf{Flexibility:} The framework easily accommodates multiple sensors with different update rates and measurement models.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Contributions and Paper Organization}

The main contributions of this work are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A complete mathematical derivation of the 17-dimensional ESKF for visual guidance, including the full Jacobian matrices for the image feature dynamics
    \item Implementation of the ESKF in MATLAB with support for delayed measurements
    \item Development of an interactive GUI for simulation, parameter tuning, and visualization
    \item Comprehensive documentation enabling educational use and further development
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:eskf} presents the complete ESKF formulation, including state definitions, kinematics, and the filter algorithm. Section~\ref{sec:gui} describes the interactive GUI and its features. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents simulation results demonstrating filter performance. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes with a summary and future work directions. Detailed Jacobian derivations are provided in the Appendix.

\section{Error-State Kalman Filter Formulation}
\label{sec:eskf}
%==============================================================================

This section presents the complete mathematical formulation of the Error-State Kalman Filter for our visual guidance application. We begin by defining the coordinate frames and state vectors, then develop the continuous-time kinematics and measurement models, and finally present the discrete-time filter algorithm.

\subsection{Coordinate Frames and Conventions}

The estimation problem involves several coordinate frames that must be carefully defined to avoid ambiguity:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Earth Frame ($e$):} A local navigation frame following the North-East-Down (NED) convention. The $x$-axis points North, the $y$-axis points East, and the $z$-axis points Down (toward the Earth's center). This frame serves as the inertial reference for position and velocity states.
    
    \item \textbf{Body Frame ($b$):} A frame fixed to the interceptor with the $x$-axis pointing forward along the vehicle's longitudinal axis, the $y$-axis pointing right, and the $z$-axis pointing down. The IMU measurements are expressed in this frame.
    
    \item \textbf{Camera Frame ($c$):} A frame attached to the onboard camera. The $z$-axis points along the optical axis (forward), while $x$ and $y$ axes define the image plane. The transformation from body to camera frame is assumed known and constant.
\end{itemize}

The rotation matrix $\R(\quat)$ transforms vectors from the body frame to the earth frame. Conversely, $\R^T$ (or equivalently $\R^{-1}$) transforms from earth to body frame. The rotation matrix $\R_{c/b}$ represents the fixed transformation from body frame to camera frame.

\subsection{Nominal State Vector}

The nominal state vector $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{18}$ contains all quantities needed to describe the interceptor's motion and sensor biases:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} \quat \\ \pr \\ \vr \\ \pbar \\ \bgyr \\ \bacc \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

Each component has a specific physical meaning and role in the estimation:

\textbf{Attitude Quaternion} ($\quat \in \mathbb{R}^4$): The quaternion represents the orientation of the body frame relative to the earth frame using the scalar-first convention $\quat = [q_0, q_1, q_2, q_3]^T$, where $q_0$ is the scalar part. The quaternion must satisfy the unit-norm constraint $\|\quat\| = 1$.

\textbf{Relative Position} ($\pr \in \mathbb{R}^3$): The position of the interceptor relative to the target, expressed in the earth frame. This is defined as $\pr = \mathbf{p}_{int} - \mathbf{p}_{tgt}$, so a zero relative position indicates intercept.

\textbf{Relative Velocity} ($\vr \in \mathbb{R}^3$): The velocity of the interceptor relative to the target in the earth frame, computed as $\vr = \dot{\pr}$.

\textbf{Normalized Image Coordinates} ($\pbar \in \mathbb{R}^2$): The target's position in the camera image plane, normalized by the focal length: $\bar{p}_x = p_{c,x}/p_{c,z}$ and $\bar{p}_y = p_{c,y}/p_{c,z}$, where $(p_{c,x}, p_{c,y}, p_{c,z})$ is the target position in camera coordinates.

\textbf{Gyroscope Bias} ($\bgyr \in \mathbb{R}^3$): The slowly-varying bias in the gyroscope measurements, modeled as a random walk process.

\textbf{Accelerometer Bias} ($\bacc \in \mathbb{R}^3$): The slowly-varying bias in the accelerometer measurements, also modeled as a random walk.

\subsection{Error State Vector}

The error state vector $\delta\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{17}$ represents small perturbations from the nominal state:
\begin{equation}
    \delta\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} \delta\bm{\theta} \\ \delta\pr \\ \delta\vr \\ \delta\pbar \\ \delta\bgyr \\ \delta\bacc \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

The key difference from the nominal state is that attitude error is represented by a three-parameter rotation vector $\delta\bm{\theta} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ rather than a four-parameter quaternion error. This vector represents a small rotation such that the true quaternion is related to the nominal quaternion by:
\begin{equation}
    \quat_{true} = \quat_{nom} \otimes \delta\quat(\delta\bm{\theta})
\end{equation}
where $\delta\quat$ is a small-angle quaternion constructed from $\delta\bm{\theta}$.

The remaining error states are simple additive errors: $\pr^{true} = \pr + \delta\pr$, and similarly for velocity, image coordinates, and biases.

\subsection{IMU Model and Dead-Reckoning}

The Inertial Measurement Unit provides measurements of angular velocity and specific force in the body frame. The measurement models account for both white noise and bias:
\begin{align}
    \bm{\omega}_m &= \bm{\omega}_{true} + \bgyr + \mathbf{n}_\omega \\
    \mathbf{a}_m &= \mathbf{a}_{true} + \bacc + \mathbf{n}_a
\end{align}

where $\bm{\omega}_m$ and $\mathbf{a}_m$ are the raw IMU measurements, $\bm{\omega}_{true}$ and $\mathbf{a}_{true}$ are the true angular velocity and specific force, and $\mathbf{n}_\omega$ and $\mathbf{n}_a$ are white Gaussian noise processes.

The biases themselves evolve as random walks driven by process noise:
\begin{align}
    \dot{\bgyr} &= \bm{\omega}_w, \quad \bm{\omega}_w \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \sigma_{\omega w}^2 \I_3) \\
    \dot{\bacc} &= \mathbf{a}_w, \quad \mathbf{a}_w \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \sigma_{aw}^2 \I_3)
\end{align}

The four noise parameters that characterize the IMU are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:imu_params}.

For dead-reckoning, the nominal state is propagated using the IMU measurements with the estimated biases subtracted. The attitude propagates according to quaternion kinematics, while position and velocity follow standard kinematic equations with gravity compensation.

\subsection{Measurement Models}

The ESKF incorporates two types of exteroceptive measurements to correct the dead-reckoning drift.

\subsubsection{Camera Measurement}
The camera provides measurements of the target's position in the normalized image plane. The measurement model is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{z}_{\text{img}} = \pbar + \mathbf{v}_{\text{img}}, \quad \mathbf{v}_{\text{img}} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \sigma_{img}^2 \I_2)
\end{equation}

The camera update rate is typically 30 Hz, providing frequent corrections to the image feature state. These measurements are particularly valuable for attitude estimation since changes in attitude directly affect the image coordinates.

\subsubsection{Radar Measurement}
The radar provides direct measurements of the relative position vector:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{z}_{\text{radar}} = \pr + \mathbf{v}_{\text{radar}}, \quad \mathbf{v}_{\text{radar}} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \sigma_{radar}^2 \I_3)
\end{equation}

Although the radar updates are infrequent (typically 0.5-2 Hz), they provide strong observability for the position states and help bound the overall estimation error.

\subsection{State Transition Jacobian Matrix}

The continuous-time error state dynamics can be written in the linear form:
\begin{equation}
    \delta\dot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{F}_c \delta\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{G}_c \mathbf{n}
\end{equation}

The state transition Jacobian $\mathbf{F}_c \in \mathbb{R}^{17 \times 17}$ captures how perturbations in each error state affect the rates of change of all error states. The structure of this matrix reflects the physical couplings in the system:

\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F}_c = \begin{bmatrix}
    \mathbf{F}_{\theta\theta} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & -\I_3 & \mathbf{0} \\
    \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \I_3 & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} \\
    \mathbf{F}_{v\theta} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & -\R \\
    \mathbf{F}_{\bar{p}\theta} & \mathbf{F}_{\bar{p}p} & \mathbf{F}_{\bar{p}v} & \mathbf{A}_{\bar{p}} & \mathbf{F}_{\bar{p}b_\omega} & \mathbf{0} \\
    \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} \\
    \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0}
\end{bmatrix}
\label{eq:Fc}
\end{equation}

The physical interpretation of the main blocks is as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathbf{F}_{\theta\theta} = -\skew{\bm{\omega}_m - \bgyr}$: Attitude error couples to itself through the angular velocity
    \item $-\I_3$ in position (1,5): Gyroscope bias directly affects attitude rate
    \item $\I_3$ in position (2,3): Velocity error directly drives position error rate
    \item $\mathbf{F}_{v\theta}$: Attitude error creates velocity error through incorrect gravity projection
    \item $-\R$ in position (3,6): Accelerometer bias creates velocity error in the earth frame
    \item Fourth row: Image feature dynamics couple to all kinematic states
\end{itemize}

The complete expressions for all Jacobian sub-blocks are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:jacobians}.

\subsection{Process Noise Covariance Matrix}

The noise Jacobian $\mathbf{G}_c \in \mathbb{R}^{17 \times 12}$ maps the 12-dimensional noise vector to the 17-dimensional error state rate. The noise vector is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{n} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{n}_\omega \\ \mathbf{n}_a \\ \bm{\omega}_w \\ \mathbf{a}_w \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{12}
\end{equation}

The continuous-time process noise covariance is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{Q}_c = \text{diag}\left(\sigma_{\tilde{\omega}n}^2 \I_3, \sigma_{\tilde{a}n}^2 \I_3, \sigma_{\omega w}^2 \I_3, \sigma_{aw}^2 \I_3\right)
\end{equation}

Note that the IMU noise parameters defined in the GUI directly populate this matrix, establishing a clear connection between the sensor characteristics specified by the user and the filter's process noise model.

\subsection{ESKF Algorithm}

The ESKF operates in a predict-update cycle, similar to the standard Kalman filter but with the important distinction that the nominal state and error state are treated separately.

\subsubsection{Prediction Phase}
At each IMU sample (typically 200 Hz), the filter performs the following steps:

\textbf{1. Nominal State Propagation:} The nominal state is propagated using the corrected IMU measurements:
\begin{align}
    \quat_{k+1} &= \quat_k \otimes \exp_q\left((\bm{\omega}_m - \bgyr) \Delta t\right) \\
    {\pr}_{k+1} &= {\pr}_k + {\vr}_k \Delta t + \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{a}_{ned} \Delta t^2 \\
    {\vr}_{k+1} &= {\vr}_k + \mathbf{a}_{ned} \Delta t \\
    {\pbar}_{k+1} &= {\pbar}_k + \dot{\pbar} \Delta t
\end{align}
where $\mathbf{a}_{ned} = \R(\quat)(\mathbf{a}_m - \bacc) + \mathbf{g}$ is the acceleration in the earth frame.

\textbf{2. Error State Reset:} The error state mean remains at zero since we assume the nominal state is our best estimate.

\textbf{3. Covariance Propagation:} The error covariance is propagated according to:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{P}_{k+1}^- = \mathbf{F}_d \mathbf{P}_k \mathbf{F}_d^T + \mathbf{G}_d \mathbf{Q}_c \mathbf{G}_d^T
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{F}_d$ and $\mathbf{G}_d$ are the discretized versions of $\mathbf{F}_c$ and $\mathbf{G}_c$.

\subsubsection{Update Phase}
When a measurement arrives (camera at 30 Hz, radar at 0.5 Hz), the filter performs the following:

\textbf{1. Innovation Computation:}
\begin{equation}
    \bm{\nu} = \mathbf{z} - h(\mathbf{x}^-)
\end{equation}

\textbf{2. Kalman Gain Computation:}
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{K} = \mathbf{P}^- \mathbf{H}^T (\mathbf{H}\mathbf{P}^-\mathbf{H}^T + \mathbf{R})^{-1}
\end{equation}

\textbf{3. Error State Update:}
\begin{equation}
    \delta\hat{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{K} \bm{\nu}
\end{equation}

\textbf{4. Nominal State Injection:} The error state is ``injected'' into the nominal state:
\begin{align}
    \quat^+ &= \quat^- \otimes \exp_q(\delta\bm{\theta}) \\
    \pr^+ &= \pr^- + \delta\pr \\
    \vr^+ &= \vr^- + \delta\vr \\
    \pbar^+ &= \pbar^- + \delta\pbar \\
    \bgyr^+ &= \bgyr^- + \delta\bgyr \\
    \bacc^+ &= \bacc^- + \delta\bacc
\end{align}

\textbf{5. Covariance Update:} Using the Joseph form for numerical stability:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{P}^+ = (\I - \mathbf{K}\mathbf{H}) \mathbf{P}^- (\I - \mathbf{K}\mathbf{H})^T + \mathbf{K} \mathbf{R} \mathbf{K}^T
\end{equation}

\textbf{6. Error State Reset:} After injection, the error state is reset to zero, and the covariance is adjusted if needed (for small errors, this adjustment is negligible).

\subsubsection{IMU Noise Parameters}
The IMU noise section allows users to adjust the four fundamental noise parameters that characterize the inertial sensor:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Accel Noise ($\sigma_{\tilde{a}n}$):} The standard deviation of white noise on accelerometer measurements, specified in m/s$^2$. Higher values simulate noisier, lower-quality accelerometers.
    
    \item \textbf{Gyro Noise ($\sigma_{\tilde{\omega}n}$):} The standard deviation of white noise on gyroscope measurements, specified in rad/s. This directly affects attitude estimation accuracy.
    
    \item \textbf{Accel Bias RW ($\sigma_{aw}$):} The accelerometer bias random walk coefficient, representing how quickly the bias drifts over time. Higher values indicate less stable sensors.
    
    \item \textbf{Gyro Bias RW ($\sigma_{\omega w}$):} The gyroscope bias random walk coefficient. This is typically much smaller than the measurement noise but causes unbounded drift if not estimated.
    
    \item \textbf{IMU Frequency:} The sampling rate of the IMU in Hertz. The default is 200 Hz, which is typical for tactical-grade IMUs.
\end{itemize}

Each noise parameter is adjustable via both a slider (for quick qualitative adjustments) and an edit box (for precise numerical entry). The sliders use scientific notation display for small values like bias random walk coefficients.

Importantly, these same noise parameters are used to construct the process noise covariance matrix $\mathbf{Q}_c$ in the ESKF. This means that setting ``correct'' values (matching the simulated IMU characteristics) will result in a consistent filter, while intentionally misspecifying these values allows users to explore the effects of filter tuning mismatches.

\subsubsection{Scenario Settings}
The scenario section defines the initial conditions for both the interceptor and target:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Int Pos [N E D]:} Initial interceptor position in the NED frame, specified as three space-separated values in meters.
    
    \item \textbf{Tgt Pos [N E D]:} Initial target position in the NED frame, in meters.
    
    \item \textbf{Int v\_b:} Initial interceptor velocity in the body frame, in m/s. This allows specifying forward velocity without knowing the initial heading.
    
    \item \textbf{Tgt v\_n:} Initial target velocity in the NED frame, in m/s. Setting non-zero values creates a moving target scenario.
    
    \item \textbf{Int Euler [Y P R]:} Initial interceptor attitude specified as Yaw, Pitch, and Roll angles in degrees. This determines the initial orientation of the body frame relative to the earth frame.
    
    \item \textbf{Max Velocity:} Maximum allowed interceptor velocity, used by the guidance controller.
    
    \item \textbf{Max Body Rate:} Maximum allowed angular velocity, constraining how quickly the interceptor can maneuver.
\end{itemize}

These settings allow creating diverse simulation scenarios, from simple head-on engagements to complex pursuit scenarios with maneuvering targets.

Three preset buttons provide quick configuration of both initial covariance and initial errors:

\subsubsection{Initial 1-Sigma Values}
These fields define the diagonal elements of the initial error covariance matrix $\mathbf{P}_0$:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Position (m), Velocity (m/s), Attitude (rad), Image (normalized), Gyro Bias (rad/s), Accel Bias (m/s$^2$)
\end{itemize}

The initial covariance should reflect the user's confidence in the initial state estimate. Larger values allow the filter more flexibility to correct the initial estimate, but may cause larger transients.

\subsubsection{Initial Errors}
These fields define the actual initial estimation errors (the difference between the true and estimated initial states). Setting non-zero values here allows studying filter convergence behavior:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Position [m], Velocity [m/s], Attitude [deg], Image [norm], Gyro Bias [rad/s], Accel Bias [m/s$^2$]
\end{itemize}

The relationship between initial covariance and initial error is important for filter consistency. Ideally, the initial error should be within the $3\sigma$ bounds implied by the initial covariance.

\section{Observability Analysis}
\label{sec:observability}
%==============================================================================

Understanding which states are observable under different sensor configurations is crucial for filter design and sensor selection. This section presents a systematic observability analysis using the observability Gramian and its Singular Value Decomposition (SVD).

\subsection{Observability Gramian Method}

The observability of a linear time-varying system can be analyzed through the observability Gramian. For our discretized ESKF error-state dynamics, the observability Gramian is constructed as:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{L} = \sum_{k=0}^{N} \bm{\Phi}_k^T \mathbf{H}^T \mathbf{H} \bm{\Phi}_k
\end{equation}
where $\bm{\Phi}_k = \mathbf{F}_{d,k-1} \mathbf{F}_{d,k-2} \cdots \mathbf{F}_{d,0}$ is the accumulated state transition matrix from time $0$ to $k$, and $\mathbf{H}$ is the measurement Jacobian.

The Gramian $\mathbf{L}$ is a symmetric positive semi-definite matrix that captures how measurement information propagates through the system dynamics. States that contribute to the null space of $\mathbf{L}$ are unobservable.

\subsection{SVD Decomposition and Observability Measure}

To quantify observability, we apply Singular Value Decomposition to the Gramian:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{L} = \mathbf{U} \bm{\Sigma} \mathbf{V}^T
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{U}$ contains the left singular vectors (orthonormal basis in state space), $\bm{\Sigma} = \text{diag}(\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \ldots, \sigma_{17})$ contains the singular values in descending order, and $\mathbf{V}$ contains the right singular vectors.

The singular values provide direct insight into observability:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Large singular values ($\sigma_i \gg 0$) indicate strongly observable directions
    \item Near-zero singular values ($\sigma_i \approx 0$) indicate unobservable or weakly observable directions
    \item The condition number $\kappa = \sigma_1 / \sigma_{17}$ indicates overall observability quality
\end{itemize}

To determine observability of individual error states, we compute a state-wise observability measure:
\begin{equation}
    \mu_i = \sqrt{\sum_{j=1}^{17} \sigma_j^2 \cdot u_{ij}^2}
\end{equation}
where $u_{ij}$ is the $(i,j)$ element of the $\mathbf{U}$ matrix. This measure quantifies how strongly each state $i$ participates in the observable subspace.

\subsection{Observability Results by Sensor Configuration}

We analyze three sensor configurations to understand the contribution of each sensor to system observability.

\subsubsection{Camera + Radar (Full Sensor Suite)}

When both camera and radar measurements are available, the system is \textbf{fully observable}. All 17 singular values of the observability Gramian are significantly above the numerical threshold ($\sigma_i > 10^{-10}$), as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:obs_full}.

    \caption{Observability analysis with both camera and radar enabled. All error states are observable, with the condition number indicating good overall observability.}
    \label{fig:obs_full}

\subsubsection{Camera Only (Radar Disabled)}

When only the camera is available, several states become unobservable:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Relative Position ($\delta\pr$):} Fully unobservable. The camera provides bearing information (direction to target) but cannot measure range. Without range information, the absolute position cannot be determined.
    \item \textbf{Relative Velocity ($\delta\vr$):} The component of velocity along the camera's $z$-axis (optical axis) is unobservable. Velocity components perpendicular to the optical axis can be inferred from image motion.
\end{itemize}

This result is intuitive: a monocular camera observes only angles, not distances. The depth ambiguity propagates to position states and velocity components along the viewing direction.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Image Features ($\delta\pbar$):} Fully unobservable. Without camera measurements, there is no direct observation of the normalized image coordinates.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Implications for Filter Design}

The observability analysis provides important guidance for filter design and sensor selection:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Sensor complementarity:} Camera and radar provide complementary information. Camera gives bearing; radar gives range. Together, they achieve full observability.
    \item \textbf{Degraded operation:} If one sensor fails, the filter can continue with degraded accuracy, but the unobservable states will drift.
    \item \textbf{Initial covariance:} For unobservable states, the initial covariance should be set conservatively, as the filter cannot reduce uncertainty in those directions.
    \item \textbf{Bias observability:} IMU biases become observable through sufficient excitation (dynamic motion) combined with exteroceptive measurements.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

This paper presented a comprehensive implementation of an Error-State Kalman Filter for visual guidance applications. The main contributions include:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A complete mathematical formulation of the 17-dimensional ESKF with image-based visual servoing features, including explicit derivation of all Jacobian matrices.
    
    \item A MATLAB implementation with support for multi-rate sensor fusion and delayed measurements.
    
    \item An interactive GUI that enables real-time simulation, parameter tuning, and visualization without requiring users to write code.

    \item Extension to handle target maneuvers via adaptive estimation
    \item Real hardware integration for experimental validation
\end{itemize}

\section{Detailed Jacobian Derivations}
\label{app:jacobians}
%==============================================================================

\subsection{Nominal State Kinematics}
The continuous-time nominal state kinematics are:
\begin{align}
    \dot{\quat} &= \frac{1}{2} \quat \otimes \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ \bm{\omega}_m - \bgyr \end{bmatrix} \\[8pt]
    \dot{\pr} &= \vr \\[8pt]
    \dot{\vr} &= \R(\quat)(\mathbf{a}_m - \bacc) + \mathbf{g} \\[8pt]
    \dot{\pbar} &= \mathbf{L}_s \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{v}_c \\ \bm{\omega}_c \end{bmatrix} \\[8pt]
    \dot{\bgyr} &= \mathbf{0} \\[8pt]
    \dot{\bacc} &= \mathbf{0}
\end{align}

\subsection{IBVS Interaction Matrix}
The image feature rate is given by:
\begin{equation}
    \dot{\pbar} = \mathbf{L}_v \mathbf{v}_c + \mathbf{L}_\omega \bm{\omega}_c
\end{equation}

where the velocity and angular parts of the interaction matrix are:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{L}_v &= \begin{bmatrix}
        -\frac{1}{p_{c,z}} & 0 & \frac{\bar{p}_x}{p_{c,z}} \\[4pt]
        0 & -\frac{1}{p_{c,z}} & \frac{\bar{p}_y}{p_{c,z}}
    \end{bmatrix} \\[10pt]
    \mathbf{L}_\omega &= \begin{bmatrix}
        \bar{p}_x \bar{p}_y & -(1+\bar{p}_x^2) & \bar{p}_y \\[4pt]
        (1+\bar{p}_y^2) & -\bar{p}_x \bar{p}_y & -\bar{p}_x
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{align}

\subsection{Complete Jacobian Sub-blocks}
The sub-blocks of the state transition Jacobian $\mathbf{F}_c$ are:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{F}_{\theta\theta} &= -\skew{\bm{\omega}_m - \bgyr} \\[6pt]
    \mathbf{F}_{\theta b_\omega} &= -\I_3 \\[6pt]
    \mathbf{F}_{v\theta} &= -\R \skew{\mathbf{a}_m - \bacc} \\[6pt]
    \mathbf{F}_{v b_a} &= -\R \\[6pt]
    \mathbf{F}_{\bar{p}\theta} &= \mathbf{L}_v \R_{c/b} \skew{\R^T \vr} + \mathbf{A}_{p_{c,z}} \left(-\mathbf{e}_3^T \R_{c/b} \skew{\R^T \pr}\right) \\[6pt]
    \mathbf{F}_{\bar{p}p} &= \mathbf{A}_{p_{c,z}} \left(-\mathbf{e}_3^T \R_{c/b} \R^T\right) \\[6pt]
    \mathbf{F}_{\bar{p}v} &= \mathbf{L}_v \R_{c/b} \R^T \\[6pt]
    \mathbf{F}_{\bar{p}b_\omega} &= \mathbf{L}_\omega (-\R_{c/b})
\end{align}

\subsection{Noise Jacobian $\mathbf{G}_c$}
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{G}_c = \begin{bmatrix}
    -\I_3 & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} \\
    \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} \\
    \mathbf{0} & -\R & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} \\
    \mathbf{L}_\omega(-\R_{c/b}) & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} \\
    \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \I_3 & \mathbf{0} \\
    \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & \I_3
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Measurement Jacobians}
The measurement Jacobians for the error state are:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{H}_{\text{img}} &= \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{0}_{2\times 3} & \mathbf{0}_{2\times 3} & \mathbf{0}_{2\times 3} & \I_2 & \mathbf{0}_{2\times 3} & \mathbf{0}_{2\times 3} \end{bmatrix} \\[8pt]
    \mathbf{H}_{\text{radar}} &= \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{0}_{3\times 3} & \I_3 & \mathbf{0}_{3\times 3} & \mathbf{0}_{3\times 2} & \mathbf{0}_{3\times 3} & \mathbf{0}_{3\times 3} \end{bmatrix}
\end{align}
